The Numbers Behind Electronic Music's Gender Problem

The UK's electronic music scene has a diversity problem

Electronic or dance music is a group of music genres that are created and produced by using electronic or electromechanical instruments. Equipment used to make such music includes electronic oscillators, synthesisers, electronic pianos or guitars, computers and music making software. The genre has evolved with the advent of new technologies from the 19th century to present day.



It has become a global phenomenon, developing into a vast and diverse industry, encompassing many subgenres and cultures in the UK. Dance music has become a network of artists, producers, DJs, organisations like record labels and distribution agencies, as well as a large part of nightlife culture, including clubs, pubs and festivals.

The UK music industry grossed £8 billion in the UK, a 5% increase in figures since the previous year and employed more than 200,000 as of 2024. In 2025, the UK Electronic Music Report found a £2.5 billion economic contribution from electronic music, a 3% increase. Comparing UK and international markets, the UK's music exports in recorded music and music publishing showed an 8% growth between 2024 and 2025.

There have been rapid developments in music genres invented or developed in the UK as well. The consumption of such genres is increasing worldwide, according to the report, making the electronic music industry – as part of a large sector – a widely consumed and abundant area for employment.

The report also finds a 10.5% increase in music events yearly, with 29,499 in 2024 to 32,601 events in 2025, evidencing the growth of this style of music among audiences within recent years. Electronic music and the stakeholders involved are, therefore, becoming a part of an international million-dollar industry, with an audience that is growing.

The Women Musicians Insight Report shows demographic breakdowns of different self-reported genders. Women tend to have little security over income in music sectors, especially due to the prevalence of freelancers. Despite the Equality Act protecting individuals against discrimination, the law still requires proof of an employment contract or a ‘contract personally to do work’, which is something freelancers may be employed outside of.

Moreover, with men earning slightly more overall, their average annual income was reported to be £21,750. In contrast, women earn £19,850 on average. Other reports have also found underrepresentation of women in this sector in earlier years.

Only 19% of women earn above £70,000 while 79% of men earn higher figures, showing a greater disparity in high income workers or people in senior roles. While such a significant gap does indicate to the presence of income inequalities, the problem is more complex. Men also make up the highest proportion of the lowest earners (less than £7,000) each year from music. This may indicate toward men being more likely to have other sources of income in order to maintain their music career.

TThe Musicians' Union found most respondents had been victims of misogyny or sexism (90%), in a survey totalling 80 female and non-binary respondents. Almost half of them (48%) had been sexually harasses while working in the industry, most who experienced it didn’t report it (85%), and over half had witnesses it (56%).

Anecdotal accounts involve reports of individuals being humiliated in public, isolated, and ridiculed in front of colleagues on account of their gender. There were also accounts of sexual harassment by colleagues, employers and audience members, ranging from sexually explicit jokes, unwanted touching, being showed videos of an explicit nature to assault and rape. Rates of reporting sexual harassment also tend to remain low in the music industry, according to a 2024 report by the Women and Equalities Committee called ‘Misogyny in Music’. Victims or witnesses who do report such behaviour are at risk of being made redundant or blacklisted. Some have even reported being hit with legal action, with the guilty company agreeing to drop the suits if the individual signs an NDA. The report makes recommendations for the Government to enforce laws banning the use of NDAs in situations of sexual harassment or violence.


Thus, reports consistently document the discrimination faced by those working in the music industry based on what they look like, their gender, showing the varied barriers in safety, income, upward progression or even treatment of individuals identifying as female or non-binary. Bringing in intersectional experiences of black female artists adds yet another layer to the rampant discrimination in the music sector. 43% of black female artists had changed something about themselves and 70% felt they had to change something about themselves – like wanting to lighten their skin – in the industry. They report feeling pigeon-holed into a genre or type of appearance, and 60% of respondents in a 2017 survey said white British people are favoured over other ethnicities. Within this industry that has fallen prey to predatory business and behaviour are the Black, other People of Colour and/or queer producers, DJs and audiences that were predominantly at the forefront of dance music scenes at its conception in cities like Chicago.

Discrimination on the basis of gender can look like remarks around or assumptions that attractive women are only employed or successful due to their looks, discrimination against women faced with pregnancy or maternity, workplace harassment bullying, inadequate facilities or structures to provide or protect women. Venues often lack amenities like changing rooms and can even be uneducated on what they can to do keep their female employees safe. The 2024 report by the Women and Equalities Committee recommends training programs for venues, stating that some countries like France require music venues to be licensed in sexual harassment and violence. Furthermore, there needs to be a central organisation with the power to enforce safety and other regulations in music.

The FACTS report is a volunteer-run project started by female:pressure – an initiative and transnational network of individuals from a diverse array of genders, and occupations in music and the arts. FACTS is its sixth edition and has been updating since 2013.

The 2013 report found that only 10% of acts at electronic music festivals worldwide were women. The edition released in 2024 spanning 56,457 artists from 1008 festivals in the time span of the two previous years. Among worldwide results, the 2024 report found female acts to be in the minority (29.8%) but the total number of women at music festivals has experienced a steady increase since 2012, with the number of male artists decreasing.

In the UK, however, an analysis of UK festival data shows a relative picture. The share of male artists has, indeed, decreased over time but still occupy the majority of acts at most festivals. Non-binary and mixed percentages see negligible changes at most festivals in representation. Moreover, as the average number of acts at UK music festivals increases, the share of female and non-binary artists decreases while numbers on male artists shows an upward trend.

At a time when female representation and participation at all levels of the music industry – be it as engineers, producers or artists – is at an all-time high, the need for regulations and steps to protect artists – especially female, trans, non-binary and queer artists – is at an all time high. The majority of men present in music roles in roles of seniority or A&R (or artists & repertoire responsible for scouting, financing, and overseeing the development of artists) have a knock-on effect. Moreover, the lack of basic safety – as an artist or audience member – at music events or workplaces in the music sector is negatively impacting the nature of music itself.

Recommendations for the progression of gender representation in UK dance music, released in 2022 by the Jaguar Foundation detail the need for fair and flexible working arrangements, transparency on the part of organisations like record labels, formation of third-party associations and committees protecting people with a Code of Conduct and enforcement of guidelines, accessibility of resources, awareness and education building, insistence of fair contract agreements, representation and more. While the music industry can greatly improve, serious steps on the part of audience members, artists and employees, organisations like labels, agencies, and the Government can be taken to create an enjoyable space in music for people of all genders and identities.
